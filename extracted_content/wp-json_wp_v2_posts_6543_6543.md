    Open\-ended question definition\<\\/h2\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                            ----------------------------------------------------

    Open questions allow for \\u2018free form\\u2019 answers, where participants respond in their own words, at a longer length. Typically, these questions start with \\u2018How\\u2019, \\u2018Tell, and \\u2018Why\\u2019\.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n

    Those responses are usually sentences, lists, or stories that provide descriptive accounts of a participant\\u2019s opinion or experience.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n

    ### Closed\-ended questions\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\n

    [Close\-ended questions\<\\/a\> can be answered with \\u2018yes\\u2019, \\u2018no\\u2019 or with a short piece of specific information. The answer options are usually multiple\-choice but can also be ratings or Likert scales.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n

    These question types collect concise statistical data that you can easily analyze.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n

    ### Open\-ended vs. closed\-ended questions\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\n

    Think of these as \\u2018tasks\\u2019 with different outputs. Close\-ended questions ask for a single word answer (or short pieces of information) to draw conclusions based on statistical analysis.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n

    Open questions require more meaningful answers based on participants\\u2019 personal experiences. You would then identify patterns and trends in the responses.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n

                                    Advantages of open\-ended questions\<\\/h2\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                        \\n- Open\-ended questions require more in\-depth answers. Meaning you collect more meaningful results.\<\\/li\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                        - You may collect feedback you wouldn\\u2019t otherwise have anticipated. E.g. problems with a product\\/service.\<\\/li\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                        - The information allows you to build profiles on your target population. E.g. demographics, hopes and fears.\<\\/li\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                        - No restrictions on the length or complexity of a participant’s answer.\<\\/li\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                        - Even with a smaller research population, you\\u2019d still collect ample data for analysis and discussion.\<\\/li\>\\n\<\\/ul\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                            Disadvantages of open\-ended questions\<\\/h2\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                \\n* Open questions can be more time consuming for participants, meaning they\\u2019re more likely to experience [survey fatigue\<\\/a\>.\<\\/li\>\\n\\n\\n\\n* Analyzing responses also takes more time and effort.\<\\/li\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                * The results are generally qualitative, meaning you cannot generalize them to a wider population.\<\\/li\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                * If your question is framed or worded incorrectly, there is a risk of influencing answer choices. E.g. Leading questions, loaded words and phrases.\<\\/li\>\\n\<\\/ul\>\\n\\n\\n\\nTo ensure your questions are as neutral as possible, see our tips for [writing survey questions\<\\/a\>.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    Tips for using open\-ended questions\<\\/h2\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    1\. Don’t write leading questions\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nThe whole point of an open question is to allow respondents to write a unique answer. If you influence their response in any way, your data will not be accurate.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    2\. Ensure all questions are necessary\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nDraft a list of questions and identify those most important to meeting your research goals. You\\u2019ll want your questionnaire to be as short as possible, or you risk taxing participants. Which will lead to dishonesty or drop\-outs.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    3\. Use open\-ended and close\-ended questions\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nThis will provide you with both statistical and inferential data. Giving you a fuller understanding of your target population and two sources of data to draw from in your conclusion.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    4\. Avoid \\u2018Why?\\u2019 questions\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nNot all respondents can provide a reason for something, or may not be aware of their reasoning for things. In these cases, respondents can make up reasons without necessarily believing them. This will negatively impact your results.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    5\. Don\\u2019t write questions that elicit a one\-word response\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nWhen writing your questions, frame them in a way that requires a story rather than a one or two\-word answer. The more engaging a question is, the more likely you are to receive a genuine response.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    Analyzing open questions with \\u2018Bucketing\\u2019\<\\/h2\>\\n\\n\\n\\n\\u2018Bucketing\\u2019 is a useful method for sorting qualitative data into categories for analysis.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    1\. Read every answer\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nNaturally, evaluating open feedback can be time\-consuming. However, it is essential to understand the full scope of opinions and experiences detailed in your data.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    2\. Identify trends and patterns\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nSort similar responses into categories (or \\u2018Buckets\\u2019\) for each of your questions. These could be as simple as \\u2018Positive Opinions\\u2019, \\u2018Neutral Opinions\\u2019, and \\u2018Negative Opinions\\u2019\. This is also known as multi\-coding.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\nIt\\u2019s fine if a response goes in more than one Bucket, as multiple ideas or opinions may be expressed. But each response must be in at least one category.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    3\. Create sub\-categories (optional)\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nIf you have a wide range of opinions expressed in one Bucket, we\\u2019d suggest you create sub\-categories.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\nThis will make your analysis more manageable.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    4\. Review each category\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nOnce every response has been placed into a Bucket, decide if you should combine or split any. Each bucket should clearly illustrate a trend or pattern in your results.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\nIf you\\u2019ve also used closed questions, they should be used to provide context to open feedback and trends.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
                                                    5\. Write a summary of the major trends\<\\/h3\>\\n\\n\\n\\nThere should be some meaningful correlation to the trends in your responses you can use to summarize your results. This summary can be supported by words\\/ quotes from respondents.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\nOnce this is done, your conclusion should be clear. Whether it\\u2019s to action changes to product development or to improve web page usability.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\nIf the above method of analyzing open feedback isn\\u2019t quite right for you, take a look at this article from research methodology on [qualitative data analysis\<\\/a\>.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\nThe rich data you collect from open\-ended questions allows you to better understand your target audience. This can be a valuable asset for all types of research. E.g. Open feedback from market research surveys can inform everything from product development to customer service processes.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\nWhen choosing between close or open\-ended questions, it doesn\\u2019t have to be one or the other. They work better when used together.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\nBegin each line of questioning with a close\-ended question, then follow it up with an open ended question.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\nThis way you not only gather quantitative data, but also unique answers that give context to answer choices.\<\\/p\>\\n\\n\\n\\n
